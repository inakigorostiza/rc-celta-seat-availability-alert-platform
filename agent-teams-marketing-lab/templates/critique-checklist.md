# Campaign Critique Checklist

After your Agent Team finishes, review the deliverables with a marketer's eye. Use this checklist to spot weak points and practice the most valuable skill: **recognizing what's generic, what's undercooked, and what's actually strong.**

AI teams produce confident-sounding work. Your job is to read it like an editor.

---

## RESEARCH.MD — Competitive Analysis

### Does the researcher identify real gaps or just list features?

- [ ] **Weak**: "Red Bull has high caffeine, Monster has more sugar, IE-Nergy is natural"
  - This is a feature comparison, not insight
- [ ] **Strong**: "Red Bull owns 'extreme performance,' Monster owns 'transgressive lifestyle,' but no one owns 'clear thinking'—that's whitespace IE-Nergy can claim"

**Questions to ask:**
- What did they *not* say about competitors' positioning? (This matters more than what they did say.)
- Did they find a genuine, ownable gap, or just a feature advantage?
- Is the gap big enough to build a brand on?

### Does the competitor analysis cover the right competitors?

- [ ] **Weak**: Only analyzing Red Bull and Monster (the giants)
- [ ] **Strong**: Also analyzing Celsius and Tenzing (the real threat — they're positioned similarly to IE-Nergy)

**Questions to ask:**
- Did they analyze direct competitors (other energy drinks) or adjacent ones (nootropics, coffee, sports drinks)?
- Did they include at least one challenger brand, not just incumbents?
- Is there a competitor they missed that should be analyzed?

### Is the insight actionable?

- [ ] **Weak**: "There is an opportunity in the health-conscious segment"
- [ ] **Strong**: "All competitors use aggressive, hyper-masculine language. IE-Nergy should be the calm, focused alternative — even in visual design (minimal, clean aesthetic vs. loud, neon branding)"

**Questions to ask:**
- Does the insight tell the Strategist what to DO?
- Can the Copywriter actually use it?
- Or is it too vague to act on?

## STRATEGY.MD — Positioning & Messaging

### Is the positioning statement actually different?

- [ ] **Weak**: "IE-Nergy is a premium, natural energy drink for active young adults"
  - This is generic. Red Bull could say this. Celsius could say this.
- [ ] **Strong**: "IE-Nergy is the energy drink for the thinking person—designed for sustained focus, not aggressive hype; for students and creators, not athletes or partiers"
  - This is specific. It defines who we're FOR and who we're NOT for.

**Questions to ask:**
- Could a competitor claim this positioning? (If yes, it's not ownable.)
- Does it reject something explicitly? (The best positioning says what you're NOT.)
- Is it defensible? (Can we credibly claim it, or are we overreaching?)

### Do the 3 key messages reinforce the positioning, or drift away?

- [ ] **Weak**: 
  - Message 1: "Natural ingredients"
  - Message 2: "Zero sugar"
  - Message 3: "B-vitamins for energy"
  - (These are features. They could apply to 10 different brands.)
  
- [ ] **Strong**:
  - Message 1: "Clean focus without the crash or jitters"
  - Message 2: "Designed for studying, not partying"
  - Message 3: "The smart person's energy"
  - (These reinforce the positioning. They're unique to IE-Nergy.)

**Questions to ask:**
- Do the messages say WHY someone buys IE-Nergy, or just WHAT is in it?
- Could Celsius or Red Bull use these messages? (If yes, they're not ownable.)
- Do they ladder up to the positioning statement, or contradict it?

### Is the channel mix justified?

- [ ] **Weak**: "Use TikTok and Instagram because that's where young people are"
  - This is obvious.
- [ ] **Strong**: "Lead with TikTok (viral discovery, peer recommendation culture), then Instagram (for aesthetic credibility and lifestyle context), then Spotify audio ads (high-intent moment during study sessions). Avoid traditional media—media budget would go to paid social, not broadcast"
  - This connects channel choice to campaign strategy and audience behavior.

**Questions to ask:**
- Did they explain WHY each channel, or just list channels?
- Is there a strategic order (lead with TikTok for awareness, convert on Instagram)?
- Did they exclude any channels? (Knowing what NOT to use is as important as what to use.)

### Are the guardrails clear and useful?

- [ ] **Weak**: "Don't use clichés" or "Be authentic"
  - Too vague to guide a copywriter.
- [ ] **Strong**: 
  - "Do NOT use: 'fuel your day,' 'crush your goals,' 'unleash,' 'optimize,' 'unlock potential,' 'biohack'"
  - "Do NOT compare to competitors on features ('unlike Red Bull, we have...')"
  - "Do NOT position as wellness/supplement—position as category challenger within energy drinks"
  - This is actionable. A copywriter can follow this.

**Questions to ask:**
- Could a copywriter actually use these guardrails?
- Are they specific to IE-Nergy or generic?
- Do they prevent bad work?

## COPY.MD — Creative Execution

### Does the Instagram caption sound like Instagram, or like a press release?

- [ ] **Weak**: "IE-Nergy is the new energy drink designed for clean, sustained focus. Our natural ingredients and zero-sugar formulation provide the perfect support for your daily activities. Available now at select retailers."
  - This reads like a brand claiming features.
  
- [ ] **Strong**: "not your parents' energy drink. (not the gym bro's either.) for the 2am essay, the all-day deep work, the back-to-back meetings where you actually need to think. yuzu citrus, wild berry, matcha mint. €2.50 at [link]"
  - This sounds like a peer recommendation. It hints at positioning (what we're NOT) without claiming it.

**Questions to ask:**
- Does it sound like a person or a brand?
- Does it use the brand voice defined in the brief?
- Is it specific (mentions flavors, price) or generic (talks about "benefits")?
- Would you scroll past this or stop?

### Does the TikTok script sound like a TikTok or a 15-second TV spot?

- [ ] **Weak**: 
  - [Shot of can] "IE-Nergy. Clean focus."
  - [Shot of student] "Designed for studying."
  - [Shot of professional] "For deep work."
  - [Can lifestyle shot] "IE-Nergy. The energy drink for focus."
  - (This is a TV script compressed. It's flat.)
  
- [ ] **Strong**:
  - [POV: sitting at desk at 2am, can of IE-Nergy]
  - "red bull makes you feel like you're about to explode"
  - [cut to: calm, focused]
  - "we just want to think"
  - [can reveal with voiceover] "matcha mint. yuzu. wild berry. €2.50"
  - [on-screen text] "for the thinking person"
  - (This uses TikTok's language—irony, POV, edit rhythm. It feels native.)

**Questions to ask:**
- Is there a hook in the first 2 seconds?
- Does it use TikTok's visual language (quick cuts, POV, on-screen text, meme-like structure)?
- Is the voiceover conversational or formal?
- Does it feel like a brand ad or like something a friend made?

### Is the email good enough to actually send?

- [ ] **Weak**: 
  - Subject: "Introducing IE-Nergy — The Clean Energy Drink"
  - Body: "We are thrilled to announce the launch of IE-Nergy, the energy drink for the discerning consumer..."
  - (Generic. Could be any brand.)
  
- [ ] **Strong**:
  - Subject: "the energy drink we actually want to drink"
  - Body: "red bull + monster are for one kind of person. we're not for those people. we're for the 2am essay, the 10-hour research session, the day when you need to actually think. it's matcha mint, wild berry, or yuzu citrus. €2.50/can. first 100 people from this email get [offer]. "
  - (Specific. Positions against competitors. Offers concrete value.)

**Questions to ask:**
- Does the subject line make you want to open it?
- Is the body about the brand or about the reader?
- Does it include a specific call to action?
- Could this actually drive trial?

### Does the search ad work within constraints?

- [ ] **Weak**: 
  - Headline: "IE-Nergy Energy Drink" (30 chars)
  - Description: "Natural energy drink with caffeine and B-vitamins. Zero sugar. Available online and at retailers. Shop now." (90 chars)
  - (Generic. Not differentiated from Red Bull ads.)
  
- [ ] **Strong**:
  - Headline: "Energy for the Thinker" (22 chars)
  - Description: "No crash. No jitters. Just calm focus. Matcha mint, wild berry, yuzu. €2.50 at [link]. Free shipping on first order." (90 chars)
  - (Ownable. Addresses a specific person. Includes positioning.)

**Questions to ask:**
- Does the headline differentiate IE-Nergy or could it be any energy drink?
- Does the description speak to the target audience's actual job-to-be-done?
- Is there a specific offer or call to action?

### Is there consistency across channels?

- [ ] **Weak**: 
  - Instagram: "Join the focus revolution"
  - TikTok: "Energy for students"
  - Email: "Premium natural ingredients"
  - (Each channel says something different. There's no core message.)
  
- [ ] **Strong**:
  - Instagram: "not your parents' energy drink" (tone: witty, peer-to-peer)
  - TikTok: "we just want to think" (tone: peer-to-peer)
  - Email: "for the 2am essay, the all-day deep work" (tone: peer-to-peer, specific examples)
  - (Same core message adapted per channel, not reinvented.)

**Questions to ask:**
- Is the brand voice consistent across channels?
- Did the copywriter adapt the message to each channel, or just copy-paste?
- Would a customer recognize this as the same brand across all channels?

## CAMPAIGN-SUMMARY.MD — Lead's Synthesis

### Does the lead make a clear recommendation, or hedge?

- [ ] **Weak**: "All three approaches have merit. Each channel has potential. The team has provided good starting material that could be refined further..."
  - This is non-committal. It doesn't help.
  
- [ ] **Strong**: "Lead with TikTok and campus activations in week 1 (viral discovery + trial). Shift to Instagram in week 2-3 (credibility + retention). Add Spotify audio ads in week 3+ (capture high-intent study sessions). Hold search until week 4 when we have enough customer data to optimize. This sequence matches audience journey: discovery → evaluation → conversion → retention."
  - This is a clear, defensible plan.

**Questions to ask:**
- Can you actually execute this recommendation?
- Does it account for timing and resource constraints?
- Is it a plan or just a summary?

### Does the lead address the key tradeoffs?

- [ ] **Weak**: No mention of budget, timeline, or resources

- [ ] **Strong**: "The 'calm focus' positioning is ownable but narrower than competitor positioning (which gives them bigger TAM). We're choosing focus over reach, betting that we'll win harder in a smaller segment. That affects channel spend—concentrate on where our niche spends time (TikTok, campus, co-working) rather than spreading across broad platforms."

**Questions to ask:**
- Did the lead call out what we're giving up?
- Are they aware of the positioning's narrowness?
- Did they make a judgment call or just summarize?

### Does the lead tie research, strategy, and creative together?

- [ ] **Weak**: The summary is three separate sections (research findings, strategy, creative) with no connections

- [ ] **Strong**: "The researcher found that no competitor owns 'clarity without crash.' The strategist built positioning around that. The copywriter executed by showing what we're NOT (aggressive, hyped) before showing what we ARE (calm, focused). All three feed each other."

**Questions to ask:**
- Can you see how each piece builds on the others?
- Is there logic to the sequence?
- Does it feel like a coherent campaign or three separate deliverables?

## Overall Campaign Assessment

### Is this a real campaign or a placeholder?

Ask yourself: **If I had a $50K budget and four weeks, could I launch this?**

- [ ] **Weak**: The positioning is vague, the creative is generic, the plan lacks specifics
- [ ] **Strong**: The positioning is ownable, the creative is distinct, the plan is actionable with specific channels, timing, and creative

### What would I push back on?

After reviewing, jot down 2-3 things you'd tell the team to refine:

1. **Positioning**: [Specific feedback — too narrow? Too generic? Defensible?]
2. **Creative**: [Specific feedback — one channel weaker than others? Copy too safe?]
3. **Plan**: [Specific feedback — missing a channel? Wrong sequence?]

### What's actually strong?

Don't just critique. Call out what works:

1. **Strength 1**: [What did the researcher do well?]
2. **Strength 2**: [What's the strongest creative piece?]
3. **Strength 3**: [What's the most interesting strategic insight?]

## After the Critique

### Option 1: Send feedback back to the team

Post your critique in the shared task list. Ask:
- Strategist: "Is this positioning narrow enough to own?"
- Copywriter: "Make the TikTok script feel more like a meme and less like an ad"
- Lead: "Walk me through the week 1-3 timeline—which creative ships when?"

### Option 2: Refine it yourself

If the output is 70% there, it's often faster to edit than to loop the team. AI teams excel at first drafts, not infinite refinement.

### Option 3: Start fresh with a new team

If the positioning misses, spawn a new strategic team to debate it. If the copy is off, spawn a new copywriter with tighter constraints.

---

## Remember

The goal isn't perfect AI output. The goal is **learning how to evaluate marketing work with rigor.**

You're developing your eye. After this exercise, you'll read marketing (yours, competitors', AI-generated) more critically. That's the real payoff.
